# New York City, Police, Transit, and Thoughts

Dear Mayor Mamdani,

I am writing to propose a straightforward fiscal and governance reform regarding subway fare enforcement that would save New York City money, reduce unnecessary policing, and better align responsibility with authority.

At present, fare evasion is a revenue problem borne by the Metropolitan Transportation Authority, a New York State public authority. However, the primary cost of enforcement is borne by the New York City Police Department, a city agency funded by the City of New York. This structural misalignment produces a perverse and well-documented outcome: the City spends more policing fare evasion than the MTA loses from fare evasion itself.

This is not a matter of ideology or public safety “vibes.” It is a budgeting failure. The entity that controls fares and benefits from enforcement does not bear the cost of enforcing them. As a result, enforcement is overused even when it fails a basic cost-benefit test.

One possible avenue is state reimbursement for subway fare enforcement.

The most immediate and realistic fix is for New York State to reimburse New York City for NYPD resources devoted to fare enforcement in the subway system. This could include overtime, dedicated patrols, administrative processing, and related court costs.

Once enforcement costs are internalized by the MTA or the State, policy decisions would quickly become more rational. If enforcement truly pays for itself, reimbursement will confirm that. If it does not, the current strategy would be reevaluated on its actual merits rather than political optics. Either outcome improves fiscal discipline and transparency.

Barring that, honestly, the preferable option is to  transfer the MTA to city control.

If the State is unwilling to reimburse the City for enforcing a state agency’s revenue policy, the logical alternative is to realign authority entirely by placing the MTA under city control. This would ensure that fare policy, enforcement decisions, and budgetary consequences sit within the same governance structure.

I recognize that this option is politically complex and unlikely in the near term. I raise it not as a demand, but as a reminder of the underlying principle: policy authority and financial responsibility must be aligned, or inefficiency is guaranteed.

New York City should not be subsidizing state-level political priorities through its police budget. Asking the State to pay for the enforcement it demands is not radical. It is sound public finance.

Thank you for your attention, and for your leadership on issues where fiscal reality and human dignity intersect.

Respectfully,

Abby Jane
