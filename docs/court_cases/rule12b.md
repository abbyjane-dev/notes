# Thoughts on the impending Rule 12(b) Motion to Dismiss

## 12(b)(6)

Defense: "Direct public employment by a state actor is not a ‘common calling’ protected by Article IV"

>Defendants’ motion analyzes this case as though Plaintiff had asserted a Title VII–style claim to public employment or a constitutional right to be hired by a state actor. The Complaint makes no such claim. Plaintiff does not allege a right to employment, nor does he challenge Defendants’ authority to require on-campus work for positions so designated.
>
>The position at issue was expressly advertised as “remote-eligible.” Defendants were willing to consider and hire candidates who could work on campus, regardless of state residence. Plaintiff was denied only the opportunity to be considered for remote work based solely on his residence in Alabama, pursuant to a categorical policy excluding residents of certain states from remote eligibility.

>Plaintiff alleges that this residency-based exclusion from consideration for remote work burdens the right to pursue a common calling on equal terms, in violation of the Privileges and Immunities Clause of Article IV.

## Precedent

### Piper

Piper specifically rejected as a 'substantial burden':

- administrative convenience,
- speculative enforcement difficulties,
- generalized concerns about oversight,
- blanked exclusions justified by "we've always done it this way."

Categorical exclusions are suspect, and the state (or state actor) must justify why residents of certain states, as a class, pose a distinct problem.

>Defendants assert that their policy “meets the requisite standard,” but that conclusion assumes the answer to the very question presented. Under Camden and Piper, once a plaintiff plausibly alleges a residency-based burden on a common calling, the burden shifts to the state to demonstrate a substantial reason for the discrimination and a close fit between means and ends. Defendants’ motion identifies no such justification on the face of the pleadings.

## 12(b)(2): Personal Jurisdiction

>Defendants did not merely make information available on the internet; they invited and processed applications from Alabama residents through a structured, interactive system, and then applied a residency-based policy to those applicants.

>Defendants characterize their contacts with Alabama as merely passive internet activity. But the position at issue was advertised through an interactive online system that invited applicants to create accounts, submit applications, and engage in individualized review. Defendants knowingly accepted applications from Alabama residents and then applied a residency-based exclusion to those applicants. Such conduct is not “passive” under the Zippo framework and reflects purposeful engagement with the forum.

>Defendants did not merely make information available on the internet; they invited and processed applications from Alabama residents through a structured, interactive system, and then applied a residency-based policy to those applicants.

>Plaintiff does not rely on his residence or the location of injury alone, but on Defendants’ deliberate engagement with Alabama residents through an interactive application process and application of a residency-based exclusion giving rise to this claim.

>Defendants knowingly accepted applications from Alabama residents and applied a residency-based exclusion to those residents through their hiring system.

>Defendants argue that their conduct was not directed toward Alabama. But the policy at issue operates by differentiating among states, approving residents of some states for remote eligibility while categorically excluding others. In that sense, the policy is necessarily directed at states qua states, including Alabama.
